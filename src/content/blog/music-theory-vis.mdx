A musical note can carry several different kinds of information.

Its **pitch** may be important. Its relationship to a **tonal center** may be relevant. It may belong to a particular **instrumental family**, or occupy a specific **register**.

`NoteSpan` provides a compact visual representation for these different contexts.

## Tonal context

Notes can be colored relative to a chosen tonal center.

For example, in a scale centered around
<NoteSpan note="D" colorRoot="D" highlighted />, the colors of the other notes are derived from their position relative to D:

<NoteSpan note="D" colorRoot="D" highlighted />
<NoteSpan note="E" colorRoot="D" />
<NoteSpan note="F" colorRoot="D" />
<NoteSpan note="G" colorRoot="D" />
<NoteSpan note="A" colorRoot="D" />
<NoteSpan note="Bb" colorRoot="D" />
<NoteSpan note="C#" colorRoot="D" />

The `colorRoot` property makes the color system relative rather than absolute. The same pitch can therefore take on a different color when the tonal center changes.

Without specifying a root, the default coloring system is used:

<NoteSpan note="D" highlighted />
<NoteSpan note="E" />
<NoteSpan note="F" />
<NoteSpan note="G" />
<NoteSpan note="A" />
<NoteSpan note="Bb" />
<NoteSpan note="C#" />

This makes it possible to choose between a fixed pitch-based palette and a palette that reflects a particular tonal context.

## Neutral notes

Color is not always useful. Sometimes the spelling or pitch of a note is the important information, while its position in a color system would only add visual noise.

The `noColor` property provides a neutral version of the component:

<NoteSpan note="D" noColor highlighted />
<NoteSpan note="E" noColor />
<NoteSpan note="F" noColor />
<NoteSpan note="G" noColor />
<NoteSpan note="A" noColor />
<NoteSpan note="Bb" noColor />
<NoteSpan note="C#" noColor />

Neutral notes use the site's general visual language instead of the musical color palette. They can therefore be used naturally in explanatory text:

The melody begins on <NoteSpan note="C4" noColor /> and moves toward
<NoteSpan note="F#4" noColor /> before returning to the tonic.

## Notes in prose

Notes are designed to work naturally inside running text.

A dominant <NoteSpan note="G" highlighted /> tends toward
<NoteSpan note="C" />, especially when
<NoteSpan note="B" /> resolves upward.

This allows musical analysis to remain readable without separating every musical element into a diagram.

The same notation can also be used to describe a complete gesture:

<NoteSpan note="C" /> → <NoteSpan note="E" /> → <NoteSpan note="G" highlighted />

<NoteSpan note="C" /> → <NoteSpan note="E" /> → <NoteSpan note="G" highlighted />

## Highlighting important notes

The `highlighted` property gives a note additional visual weight.

This can be useful for emphasizing:

- a tonic: <NoteSpan note="C" highlighted />
- a dominant: <NoteSpan note="G" />
- a leading tone: <NoteSpan note="B" />
- an altered note: <NoteSpan note="F#" />
- a particular register: <NoteSpan note="C6" />

Highlighting is therefore independent from the note's identity. It can be used to draw attention to a note that is structurally important within a larger musical context.

## Register

Octaves are inferred directly from the note notation.

A melody can therefore be represented as:

<NoteSpan note="C4" />
<NoteSpan note="E4" />
<NoteSpan note="G4" highlighted />

The same pitch classes in different registers remain visually distinguishable:

<NoteSpan note="C2" noColor />
<NoteSpan note="C4" />
<NoteSpan note="C7" noColor highlighted />

The octave is displayed as a small secondary element, allowing the pitch class to remain the primary visual information.

## Enharmonic spellings

The component preserves the spelling of the input note, including unusual alterations.

For example, the enharmonic relationship between
<NoteSpan note="C##" /> and <NoteSpan note="D" /> can be represented directly.

Similarly, <NoteSpan note="Fbb" /> may be used when the theoretical spelling is more important than the enharmonic equivalent
<NoteSpan note="Eb" highlighted />.

Double sharps are represented using `x`:

<NoteSpan note="C##" />

while flats and double flats retain their conventional notation:

<NoteSpan note="Bb" />
<NoteSpan note="Fbb" />

This makes the component suitable for theoretical contexts where enharmonic spelling carries analytical meaning.

## Different textual contexts

Because `NoteSpan` is an inline component, it can be used in many different kinds of content.

For example, notes can appear in emphasized text:

*an emphasized <NoteSpan note="Eb" /> note*

or in strong text:

**a strong <NoteSpan note="A" highlighted /> note**

They can also appear in lists:

- Tonic: <NoteSpan note="C" highlighted />
- Dominant: <NoteSpan note="G" />
- Leading tone: <NoteSpan note="B" />
- Altered note: <NoteSpan note="F#" />

They can even be used inside links:

[Explore <NoteSpan note="C" /> major](#tonal-context)

or alongside code notation:

The note `C#` can be represented visually as
<NoteSpan note="C#" />.

The parsed value `Bb4` becomes
<NoteSpan note="Bb4" highlighted />.

## Instrumental context

The <InstrumentSpan category="strings">violins</InstrumentSpan>
sustain the melody while the
<InstrumentSpan category="brass">horns</InstrumentSpan>
reinforce the cadence.

The same harmonic material can also be viewed through the instruments that carry it.

Consider a simple C-major sonority distributed across the orchestra:

- The **cellos and basses** establish the root:
  <NoteSpan
      note="C2"
      colorBy="instrument"
      instrumentCategory="strings"
  />

- The **horns** reinforce the fifth:
  <NoteSpan
      note="G3"
      colorBy="instrument"
      instrumentCategory="brass"
  />

- The **violas** fill in the third:
  <NoteSpan
      note="E4"
      colorBy="instrument"
      instrumentCategory="strings"
  />

- The **flutes** double the upper root:
  <NoteSpan
      note="C5"
      colorBy="instrument"
      instrumentCategory="woodwinds"
  />

The resulting voicing is therefore:

<NoteSpan
    note="C2"
    colorBy="instrument"
    instrumentCategory="strings"
/>

<NoteSpan
    note="G3"
    colorBy="instrument"
    instrumentCategory="brass"
/>

<NoteSpan
    note="E4"
    colorBy="instrument"
    instrumentCategory="strings"
/>

<NoteSpan
    note="C5"
    colorBy="instrument"
    instrumentCategory="woodwinds"
/>

The pitches form a C-major sonority:

<NoteSpan note="C" colorRoot="C" highlighted />
<NoteSpan note="E" colorRoot="C" />
<NoteSpan note="G" colorRoot="C" />

while the instrumental colors reveal how that harmony is distributed through the orchestra.

The low <NoteSpan note="C2" colorBy="instrument" instrumentCategory="strings" />
provides the foundation. The <NoteSpan note="E4" colorBy="instrument" instrumentCategory="strings" />
adds the third within the string section, while the
<NoteSpan note="G3" colorBy="instrument" instrumentCategory="brass" />
gives the sonority a distinct brass reinforcement.

Finally, the upper
<NoteSpan note="C5" colorBy="instrument" instrumentCategory="woodwinds" />
provides a lighter doubling of the root.

In this context, the two color systems describe different dimensions of the same event:

- **Relative note colors** describe the harmonic relationship between pitches.
- **Instrument colors** describe the orchestral section responsible for each pitch.
- **Octaves** describe the register and spacing of the voicing.
